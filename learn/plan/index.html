Plan

Turn your aim into one aimed grid — 30 pieces, every one tagged to a ring. Read this, run the build action, then take the self-check at the bottom.

01 · One grid, not 30 ideas

Stop agonizing cell by cell

The old way: stare at a blank calendar and squeeze out 30 ideas one by one. The system way: a strategist agent fills one 30-piece grid, already aimed at your bullseye. Pillars → grid → calendar.

The plan comes back as a draft you direct, not a wish list you stare at. You don't fill the cells — you review them.

02 · Ring-tag every piece

The tag that closes the loop

Every piece in the grid carries a tag: which ring of your bullseye it serves. It looks like decoration. It isn't — it's the wiring for the learning loop.

At the end of the month, analytics can read results per ring — which ring watched, which ring converted — and next month's plan re-aims on real numbers instead of guesses.

03 · Direct, don't do

Correct in one sentence, re-run

The first draft will be partly wrong. That's fine — it's a draft. Don't rewrite it yourself, and don't throw it away and start over with a new prompt.

Correct it in one sentence — "more Ring-1 how-to, fewer hot takes" — and re-run. One-sentence corrections keep you the director, not the typist. That habit is the whole skill of running a crew.

04 · Batch beats one-off

Pay the setup cost once

Making one post at a time means paying the setup cost — the aim, the voice, the format decisions — every single time. Batching pays it once and spreads it across the whole month.

The leverage is amortizing the aim + the formats across 30 pieces at once. Volume without aim is noise; a plan is "aimed" when every cell traces back to who you're for.
