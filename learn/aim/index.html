Aim

Lock who it's for and how you sound — before you produce a single piece. Read this, run the build action, then take the self-check at the bottom.

01 · Broad kills reach

Precision first. Expand later.

Every platform works the same way: it shows your post to a small test group, watches what they do, and decides who sees it next. If your content is for "everyone," that signal comes back muddy — the platform can't tell who to show it to, so it shows it to almost no one.

If your content is clearly for one kind of person, the signal is clean and distribution follows. That's why we aim before we produce anything: the algorithm rewards precision, not breadth.

Broad content confuses the distribution signal. Precision first — expand later, from a position of strength.

02 · The audience bullseye

Center, rings, and the 3/1/1 mix

Picture a target. The center is the one person your content serves best — specific enough that you could describe their day. Each ring outward is an adjacent audience: close to the center, but one step broader.

The 3/1/1 mix is how you spend a batch of content: roughly 3 parts for the center, 1 part one ring out, 1 part pure reach. Most of what you make serves the center; a little of it stretches outward so the audience keeps growing without your content going stale.

03 · Voice is a spec, not a vibe

Write it once. The system obeys it.

If you describe your voice differently every time you prompt, you drift. Instead, write it once — the words you use, the words you never use, your tone, your hard rules — and save it as a file the whole system reads.

That's your brand spec: a written constraint the agents can't break. Not a prompt you retype and drift on.

04 · Gates keep you the director

Nothing ships without your OK

A gate is a human checkpoint before anything irreversible happens — like posting. The system produces; you approve at the gates. That's the deal that makes autonomy safe: the crew does the work, you stay the director.
